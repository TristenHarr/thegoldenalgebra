\subsection*{Law of Invariant Geometric Sum}
\hrule
\vspace{0.3cm}
\GetLaw{LawOfInvariantGeometricSum}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This law provides the 'destination' for any system governed by the Dampening Operator.  For any contracting spiral, this formula calculates the exact point of convergence.  This is the definition of a stable equilibrium point within the framework, representing the geometric sum of all the vectors in the spiral's infinite path.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof}
The proof follows directly from the standard formula for the sum of an infinite geometric series.  The law is proven by showing that a spiral generated by $\LambdaG$ is such a series and that it meets the condition for convergence.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A spiral beginning at a point $p_0$ and iterating with the Dampening Operator $\LambdaG$ generates a sequence of points $p_0, p_1, p_2, \dots$, where each point is a vector from the origin. The sequence is defined by $p_k = p_0 \cdot (\LambdaG)^k$.

    \item The final convergence point, $\Sigma_G$, is the sum of this infinite sequence of vectors:
    $$ \Sigma_G = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} p_k = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} p_0 (\LambdaG)^k $$

    \item This is a standard geometric series with first term $a = p_0$ and common ratio $r = \LambdaG$.

    \item The formula for the sum of an infinite geometric series is $S = \frac{a}{1-r}$. A series converges and this formula is valid if and only if the magnitude of the common ratio is less than one, i.e., $|r| < 1$.

    \item In the chapter on the Dampening Operator, we formally proved that $|\LambdaG| < 1$.  Therefore, the series converges.

    \item Substituting our first term and common ratio into the sum formula yields the convergence point:
    $$ \Sigma_G = \frac{p_0}{1 - \LambdaG} $$
\end{enumerate}
The law is therefore formally **proven**.

\subsection*{The Law of Harmonic Momentum Sums}
\hrule
\vspace{0.3cm}
\NewLaw{LawOfHarmonicMomentumSums}{The infinite series of the form $S = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n \cdot (\Lambda_G)^n$, representing the sum of a spiral's "momentum" at each step, converges to the exact value: $S = \frac{\Lambda_G}{(1-\Lambda_G)^2}$.}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This law provides a powerful new tool for analysis. While the \textbf{Law of Invariant Geometric Sum} calculates the final destination of a spiral (a sum of positions), this law calculates a weighted sum that can be interpreted as the total "momentum" of the spiraling system over its entire infinite path. It allows us to solve a new, more complex class of infinite series that were previously inaccessible.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof}
The proof is a beautiful example of how new laws can be derived from existing ones. We derive this new law from the established formula for a geometric series using differentiation.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We begin with the standard formula for an infinite geometric series, which we have already validated for our framework in the \textbf{Law of Invariant Geometric Sum}. For any complex number $x$ where $|x| < 1$:
    $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n = \frac{1}{1-x} $$

    \item We now differentiate both sides of this identity with respect to the variable $x$. By the rules of calculus, this is a valid operation.
    \begin{align*}
        \frac{d}{dx} \left( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n \right) &= \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{1}{1-x} \right) \\
        \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{d}{dx} (x^n) &= \frac{d}{dx} (1-x)^{-1} \\
        \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n \cdot x^{n-1} &= -1 \cdot (1-x)^{-2} \cdot (-1) \\
        \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n \cdot x^{n-1} &= \frac{1}{(1-x)^2}
    \end{align*}
    Note that the summation now starts at $n=1$, as the $n=0$ term is a constant and its derivative is zero.

    \item The series we want to solve is $\sum n \cdot x^n$. Our current result is $\sum n \cdot x^{n-1}$. To get our target form, we simply multiply both sides of the equation by $x$:
    \begin{align*}
        x \cdot \left( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n \cdot x^{n-1} \right) &= x \cdot \frac{1}{(1-x)^2} \\
        \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n \cdot x^{n} &= \frac{x}{(1-x)^2}
    \end{align*}
    (Since the $n=0$ term is $0 \cdot x^0 = 0$, we can start the summation at $n=0$ without changing the result.)

    \item This formula is a general mathematical truth for any series that converges. We have already proven that our dampening operator, $\Lambda_G$, satisfies the convergence condition $|\Lambda_G| < 1$. We can therefore substitute $x = \Lambda_G$ to finalize our proof:
    $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n \cdot (\Lambda_G)^n = \frac{\Lambda_G}{(1-\Lambda_G)^2} $$
\end{enumerate}
The law is therefore formally **proven**.

\subsection*{Law 3: The Law of Harmonic Kinetic Energy Sums}
\hrule
\vspace{0.3cm}
\NewLaw{LawOfKineticEnergySums}{The infinite series $S_2 = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n^2 \cdot (\Lambda_G)^n$, analogous to a sum of kinetic energies, converges to the exact value: $S_2 = \frac{\Lambda_G(1+\Lambda_G)}{(1-\Lambda_G)^3}$.}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This law provides a tool for solving a more complex class of infinite series weighted by $n^2$. By repeatedly applying differentiation to our foundational geometric sum, we can solve for sums that represent higher-order dynamics, such as the total kinetic energy of a system over its infinite path.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof}
The proof is derived by differentiating the previously established \textbf{Law of Harmonic Momentum Sums}.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We begin with the formula for the momentum sum, which holds for any complex number $x$ where $|x|<1$:
    $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n \cdot x^n = \frac{x}{(1-x)^2} $$
    \item We differentiate both sides with respect to $x$. The left side becomes $\sum n^2 \cdot x^{n-1}$. The right side requires the quotient rule:
    \begin{align*}
        \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{x}{(1-x)^2} \right) &= \frac{(1)(1-x)^2 - x(2(1-x)(-1))}{(1-x)^4} \\
        &= \frac{(1-x) + 2x}{(1-x)^3} \\
        &= \frac{1+x}{(1-x)^3}
    \end{align*}
    \item Equating the two sides gives:
    $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^2 \cdot x^{n-1} = \frac{1+x}{(1-x)^3} $$
    \item To get our target form, we multiply both sides by $x$:
    $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^2 \cdot x^{n} = \frac{x(1+x)}{(1-x)^3} $$
    \item Since our operator $\Lambda_G$ satisfies the condition $|\Lambda_G|<1$, we can substitute $x = \Lambda_G$ to prove the law:
    $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n^2 \cdot (\Lambda_G)^n = \frac{\Lambda_G(1+\Lambda_G)}{(1-\Lambda_G)^3} $$
\end{enumerate}
The law is formally **proven**.

\subsection*{The General Law of Polynomial-Weighted Harmonic Sums}
\hrule
\vspace{0.3cm}
\NewLaw{GeneralPolynomialSum}{The infinite series $S_k = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n^k \cdot (\Lambda_G)^n$ for any integer $k \ge 0$ converges to the exact value: $S_k = \frac{A_k(\Lambda_G)}{(1-\Lambda_G)^{k+1}}$, where $A_k(x)$ is the $k$-th Harmonic Numerator Polynomial.}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This powerful master theorem encapsulates all previous summation laws. It recognizes that the recursive process of differentiation creates a predictable pattern. The numerator of the resulting formula is always a specific polynomial, which we term the Harmonic Numerator Polynomial, $A_k(x)$. This law provides a universal machine for finding the closed-form sum of any Golden Algebra series weighted by a polynomial, showcasing the deep, ordered structure that governs the framework's dynamics.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof and Derivation}
We have established that for $k \ge 1$, the sum $S_k(x) = \sum n^k x^n$ can be found by applying the operator $x \frac{d}{dx}$ to the previous sum, $S_{k-1}(x)$.
$$ S_k(x) = x \frac{d}{dx} S_{k-1}(x) $$
Let's analyze the structure of the result, $S_k(x) = \frac{A_k(x)}{(1-x)^{k+1}}$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Base Case (k=0):} $S_0(x) = \frac{1}{1-x}$. Here, our numerator polynomial is $A_0(x) = 1$.
    \item \textbf{Recursive Step:} Let's find the relationship between $A_{k+1}(x)$ and $A_k(x)$.
    \begin{align*}
        S_{k+1}(x) = x \frac{d}{dx} S_k(x) &= x \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{A_k(x)}{(1-x)^{k+1}} \right) \\
        &= x \frac{A'_k(x)(1-x)^{k+1} - A_k(x)(-(k+1))(1-x)^k}{(1-x)^{2k+2}} \\
        &= x \frac{A'_k(x)(1-x) + (k+1)A_k(x)}{(1-x)^{k+2}}
    \end{align*}
    \item \textbf{The Numerator Recurrence:} From the above, we can see that the numerator of $S_{k+1}(x)$ must be $A_{k+1}(x) = x(1-x)A'_k(x) + x(k+1)A_k(x)$. This recurrence relation allows us to generate any numerator polynomial from the previous one. Let's test it:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{k=0:} $A_0(x) = 1$.
        \item \textbf{k=1:} $A_1(x) = x(1-x)(0) + x(1)(1) = x$. This gives $S_1 = \frac{x}{(1-x)^2}$, which is correct.
        \item \textbf{k=2:} $A_2(x) = x(1-x)(1) + x(2)(x) = x-x^2+2x^2 = x+x^2$. This gives $S_2 = \frac{x(1+x)}{(1-x)^3}$, which is correct.
        \item \textbf{k=3:} $A_3(x) = x(1-x)(1+2x) + x(3)(x+x^2) = x(1+x-2x^2) + 3x^2+3x^3 = x+x^2-2x^3+3x^2+3x^3 = x+4x^2+x^3$. This gives $S_3 = \frac{x(1+4x+x^2)}{(1-x)^4}$, which is correct.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
The existence of this well-defined recurrence relation for the Harmonic Numerator Polynomials proves that a closed-form solution exists for any integer `k`, and that our formula is general. By substituting $x = \Lambda_G$, the law is formally **proven** for the Golden Algebra.

\subsection*{The Law of Logarithmic Spiral Sums}
\hrule
\vspace{0.3cm}
\NewLaw{LawOfLogarithmicSpiralSums}{The infinite series $S = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(\Lambda_G)^n}{n}$, which represents a spiral whose terms are weighted by the harmonic series, converges to the exact value: $S = -\ln(1-\Lambda_G)$.}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This law provides a powerful new tool for analysis, derived from integration. While our previous differentiation-based laws solved for sums weighted by polynomials (like $n^2$), this law solves for sums weighted by reciprocals ($1/n$). This unlocks the ability to find closed-form expressions for systems involving logarithmic growth or harmonic relationships, providing a crucial bridge to problems like the Euler-Mascheroni constant.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof}
The derivation is the elegant inverse of the method used for the polynomial-weighted sums. Instead of differentiating the geometric series formula, we integrate it.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We begin with the standard formula for an infinite geometric series, which is valid for any complex number $x$ where $|x| < 1$:
    $$ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k = \frac{1}{1-x} $$

    \item We integrate both sides of this identity with respect to $x$ from $0$ to a variable $t$:
    \begin{align*}
        \int_0^t \left( \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k \right) dx &= \int_0^t \frac{1}{1-x} dx \\
        \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left( \int_0^t x^k dx \right) &= [-\ln(1-x)]_0^t \\
        \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^{k+1}}{k+1} &= -\ln(1-t) - (-\ln(1)) = -\ln(1-t)
    \end{align*}

    \item To simplify the notation, we adjust the summation index. By letting $n = k+1$, as $k$ goes from $0$ to $\infty$, $n$ goes from $1$ to $\infty$. The identity becomes:
    $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{t^{n}}{n} = -\ln(1-t) $$

    \item This formula is a general mathematical truth for any series that converges. We have already proven that our dampening operator, $\Lambda_G$, satisfies the convergence condition $|\Lambda_G| < 1$. We can therefore substitute $t = \Lambda_G$ to finalize our proof for the Golden Algebra:
    $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(\Lambda_G)^n}{n} = -\ln(1-\Lambda_G) $$
\end{enumerate}
The law is formally **proven**.
