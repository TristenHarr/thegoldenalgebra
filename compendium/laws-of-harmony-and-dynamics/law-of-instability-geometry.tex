\subsection*{Law of Instability Geometry}
\hrule
\vspace{0.3cm}
\GetLaw{LawOfInstabilityGeometry}
\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This law demonstrates that even unstable systems possess a deep underlying order. When a system is made unstable by the repulsive constant K, its components do not fly apart randomly. Instead, they follow specific, predictable hyperbolic trajectories, revealing a hidden geometry even within chaos.

\subsubsection*{Proof}
The proof involves analyzing the eigenvectors of the transformation matrix when a repulsive component K is introduced, showing they correspond to escape vectors.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof}
To model instability, we construct a transformation matrix $G_{\text{unstable}}$ where the attractive constant $J$ is replaced by the repulsive constant $K$. We then find the eigenvalues of this matrix. If the eigenvalues are real, they correspond to non-rotational dynamics (i.e., escape trajectories).

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Let the unstable transformation matrix be defined as:
    $$ G_{\text{unstable}} = \begin{pmatrix} T & K \\ K & T \end{pmatrix} $$
    
    \item We find the eigenvalues $\lambda$ by solving the characteristic equation $\det(G_{\text{unstable}} - \lambda I) = 0$:
    \begin{align*}
        \det \begin{pmatrix} T-\lambda & K \\ K & T-\lambda \end{pmatrix} &= 0 \\
        (T-\lambda)^2 - K^2 &= 0 \\
        (T-\lambda)^2 &= K^2 \\
        T-\lambda &= \pm K \\
        \lambda &= T \mp K
    \end{align*}
    
    \item This gives two distinct eigenvalues:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\lambda_1 = T - K$
        \item $\lambda_2 = T + K$
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item From the appendix (\textbf{Property 17} and \textbf{Property 14}), we know the exact values of these combinations:
    $$ \lambda_1 = T - K = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2} $$
    $$ \lambda_2 = T + K = -\frac{1}{2} $$
    
    \item Both eigenvalues are real numbers. Unlike the complex-conjugate eigenvalues of the stable matrix $G$ which produce rotation, these real eigenvalues correspond to pure scaling along the directions of their real-valued eigenvectors. These scaling transformations produce the predictable, hyperbolic escape trajectories. The law is therefore \textbf{proven}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Computational Verification}
The following Python script symbolically calculates the eigenvalues of the unstable matrix $G_{\text{unstable}}$ and confirms that they are real, verifying the condition for non-rotational, escape-based dynamics.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Symbolic proof of the Law of Instability Geometry.}, label={lst:instability_geometry_proof}]
import sympy


def prove_instability_geometry():
    """
    Symbolically proves the Law of Instability Geometry.

    This script analyzes a transformation matrix G_unstable that includes
    the repulsive constant K. It proves that the matrix has real eigenvalues
    and eigenvectors, which correspond to non-rotational, hyperbolic escape
    trajectories.
    """
    # --- Define symbolic constants ---
    # Using the fully-substituted values for clarity in the matrix
    sqrt5 = sympy.sqrt(5)
    T = (sqrt5 - 1) / 4
    K = -(sqrt5 + 1) / 4

    print("=" * 60)
    print("Symbolic Proof of the Law of Instability Geometry")
    print("=" * 60)

    # --- Step 1: Define the Unstable Transformation Matrix ---
    # We introduce the repulsive constant K into the off-diagonal elements,
    # representing a system where repulsion is a key dynamic.
    G_unstable = sympy.Matrix([[T, K], [K, T]])

    print("1. An unstable transformation matrix, G_unstable, is defined:")
    print(G_unstable)
    print("\nNote: The repulsive constant K is on the off-diagonal.\n")

    # --- Step 2: Calculate Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors ---
    # SymPy's eigenvects() method returns a list of tuples:
    # (eigenvalue, multiplicity, [eigenvectors])
    eigen_data = G_unstable.eigenvects()

    print("2. Calculating the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of G_unstable...")

    # --- Step 3: Verify the Properties of the Eigensystem ---
    # The proof requires showing the eigenvalues are real, leading to real eigenvectors
    # and thus non-rotational dynamics.

    is_proven = True
    for eigenvalue, multiplicity, eigenvectors in eigen_data:
        print(f"\n   Eigenvalue: {eigenvalue.simplify()}")
        print(f"   Is Eigenvalue Real? {eigenvalue.is_real}")

        if not eigenvalue.is_real:
            is_proven = False

        # An eigenvector is a basis for the eigenspace.
        # We can show one of the basis vectors.
        # The eigenvector will be real if the eigenvalue is real for this matrix.
        for vector in eigenvectors:
            print(f"   Corresponding Eigenvector: {vector.simplify()}")

    print("-" * 60)

    # --- Step 4: Conclusion ---
    assert is_proven, "Proof failed: Not all eigenvalues were real."

    print("\nConclusion: The eigenvalues are real numbers, not complex conjugates.")
    print("This means the transformation is a pure stretch/compression along")
    print("the real-valued eigenvector directions, not a rotation.")
    print("This corresponds to predictable, hyperbolic escape trajectories.")
    print("The Law of Instability Geometry is computationally verified.")
    print("=" * 60)


if __name__ == '__main__':
    prove_instability_geometry()
\end{lstlisting}
