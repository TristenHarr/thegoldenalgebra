\subsubsection*{Conceptual Overview}
This law assigns clear physical meaning to the core constants J and K. It identifies J as the algebraic source of attraction, leading to stable, closed orbits. Conversely, it identifies K as the source of repulsion, leading to instability and open, escape trajectories.

\subsubsection*{Proof}
The proof analyzes the behavior of dynamical systems under the influence of J-dominant and K-dominant operators, showing convergence in the first case and divergence in the second.

\subsubsection*{Formal Proof}
The proof is established by analyzing the eigenvalues of the transformation matrices associated with J and K. An eigenvalue with magnitude less than 1 implies convergence (attraction), while an eigenvalue with magnitude greater than 1 implies divergence (repulsion).

\paragraph{Part 1: The Attractive Nature of J}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The J-dominant system is represented by the stable matrix $G = \begin{pmatrix} T & -J \\ J & T \end{pmatrix}$, as defined in the \textbf{Law of Matrix-Operator Duality}.
    \item The eigenvalues of this matrix were proven to be $\Lambda_G = T+iJ$ and its conjugate in the \textbf{Law of Harmonic Eigenvalues}.
    \item In the chapter on the \textbf{Dampening Operator}, the magnitude of these eigenvalues was formally proven to be less than 1:
    $$ |\Lambda_G| = \sqrt{T^2+J^2} \approx 0.363 < 1 $$
    \item Since all modes of transformation for the J-dominant system are convergent, this proves that \textbf{J corresponds to attraction}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Part 2: The Repulsive Nature of K}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The K-dominant system is represented by the unstable matrix $G_{\text{unstable}} = \begin{pmatrix} T & K \\ K & T \end{pmatrix}$, as defined in the \textbf{Law of Instability Geometry}.
    \item The eigenvalues of this matrix were proven to be $\lambda_1 = T-K$ and $\lambda_2 = T+K$.
    \item The magnitude of the first eigenvalue is:
    $$ |\lambda_1| = |T-K| = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.118 $$
    \item Because there exists an eigenvalue with a magnitude greater than 1, this system possesses a divergent mode. Repeated application of this transformation will result in expansion along the corresponding eigenvector.
    \item This proves that \textbf{K corresponds to repulsion}. The duality is hereby \textbf{proven}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Computational Verification}
The following Python script computationally verifies the duality by analyzing the eigenvalues of the J-dominant (stable) and K-dominant (unstable) matrices.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Symbolic proof of the Law of Duality.}, label={lst:duality_proof}]
import sympy


def prove_law_of_duality():
    """
    Symbolically proves the Law of Duality (Attraction/Repulsion).

    This script analyzes the eigenvalues of two matrices:
    1. A J-dominant matrix (the stable matrix G), to show its eigenvalues
        have a magnitude < 1, causing convergence (attraction).
    2. A K-dominant matrix (the unstable matrix), to show it has at least
        one eigenvalue with magnitude > 1, causing divergence (repulsion).
    """
    # --- Define symbolic constants ---
    sqrt5 = sympy.sqrt(5)
    T = (sqrt5 - 1) / 4
    J = (3 - sqrt5) / 4
    K = -(sqrt5 + 1) / 4

    print("=" * 60)
    print("Symbolic Proof of the Law of Duality (Attraction/Repulsion)")
    print("=" * 60)

    # --- Part 1: J-Dominant System (Attraction) ---
    print("\n--- 1. Analyzing J-Dominant System for Attraction ---")
    G_stable = sympy.Matrix([[T, -J], [J, T]])
    stable_eigenvalues = list(G_stable.eigenvals().keys())

    print(f"The stable matrix G is:\n{G_stable}\n")
    print(f"Its eigenvalues are: {stable_eigenvalues}")

    # We only need to check one eigenvalue, as they are complex conjugates
    # and thus have the same magnitude.
    stable_mag_sq = sympy.simplify(sympy.Abs(stable_eigenvalues[0]) ** 2)
    print(f"The squared magnitude of the eigenvalues is: {stable_mag_sq.evalf()}")

    assert stable_mag_sq < 1, "J-dominant system should be convergent."
    print("Result: Magnitude is < 1. This system CONVERGES (Attraction).")

    # --- Part 2: K-Dominant System (Repulsion) ---
    print("\n--- 2. Analyzing K-Dominant System for Repulsion ---")
    G_unstable = sympy.Matrix([[T, K], [K, T]])
    unstable_eigenvalues = list(G_unstable.eigenvals().keys())

    print(f"The unstable matrix G_unstable is:\n{G_unstable}\n")
    print(f"Its eigenvalues are: {unstable_eigenvalues}")

    # Check the magnitude of each real eigenvalue
    has_divergent_mode = False
    for val in unstable_eigenvalues:
        mag = sympy.Abs(val)
        print(f"Analyzing eigenvalue {val.simplify()}: Magnitude is {mag.evalf()}")
        if mag > 1:
            has_divergent_mode = True

    assert has_divergent_mode, "K-dominant system should be divergent."
    print("Result: At least one eigenvalue has magnitude > 1. This system DIVERGES (Repulsion).")

    # --- Conclusion ---
    print("\n" + "=" * 60)
    print("Conclusion: The J-dominant system is purely convergent, while the")
    print("K-dominant system possesses a divergent mode. This confirms the")
    print("duality of J as attractive and K as repulsive.")
    print("The Law of Duality is computationally verified.")
    print("=" * 60)


if __name__ == '__main__':
    prove_law_of_duality()
\end{lstlisting}
